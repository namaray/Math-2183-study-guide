Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

The "characteristic" properties that define a linear transformation.

1. The Fundamentals

In German, "eigen" means "own" or "characteristic." These values reveal the skeleton of a matrix transformation.

The Master Equation:

$Av = \lambda v$

The matrix $A$ acting on $v$ is the same as just scaling $v$ by $\lambda$.

  • Eigenvector ($v$): A non-zero vector that doesn't rotate when the matrix is applied. It stays on its original line (span).
  • Eigenvalue ($\lambda$): The magnitude of the stretch or shrink along that eigenvector's direction.

Geometric Intuition

Think of a transformation like stretching a rubber sheet. Most points move to a new direction, but points along certain lines only move further away or closer to the center. Those lines are the Eigen-directions.

2. Key Properties (Exam Favorites)

These shortcuts are great for double-checking your work:

  • Sum of Eigenvalues: $\sum \lambda = tr(A)$ (The sum of eigenvalues equals the Trace of the matrix).
  • Product of Eigenvalues: $\prod \lambda = \det(A)$ (The product of eigenvalues equals the Determinant).
  • Triangle/Diagonal Matrices: The eigenvalues are simply the entries on the main diagonal.
  • Invertibility: A matrix is invertible if and only if none of its eigenvalues are zero ($\lambda \neq 0$).

3. Worked Example: Problem 25

Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors for $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$.

Part A: Find Eigenvalues ($\lambda$)

Solve the Characteristic Equation: $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$.

$\det \begin{bmatrix} 1-\lambda & 2 \\ 2 & 4-\lambda \end{bmatrix} = 0$

$(1-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (2 \cdot 2) = 0$
$\lambda^2 - 4\lambda - 1\lambda + 4 - 4 = 0$
$\lambda^2 - 5\lambda = 0 \implies \lambda(\lambda - 5) = 0$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 5, \lambda_2 = 0$

Check: Trace $1+4=5$. Determinant $(4-4)=0$. Our eigenvalues ($5,0$) sum to 5 and multiply to 0. Correct!

Part B: Find Eigenvectors ($v$)

For each $\lambda$, solve the homogeneous system $(A - \lambda I)v = 0$.

Case 1: For $\lambda = 5$
$\begin{bmatrix} 1-5 & 2 \\ 2 & 4-5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \implies \begin{bmatrix} -4 & 2 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$

$-4x + 2y = 0 \implies 2y = 4x \implies y = 2x$.
Let $x=1$, then $y=2$.
Eigenvector $v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$
Case 2: For $\lambda = 0$
$\begin{bmatrix} 1-0 & 2 \\ 2 & 4-0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \implies \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$

$x + 2y = 0 \implies x = -2y$.
Let $y=1$, then $x=-2$.
Eigenvector $v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$
Pro-Tip: Why do we "pick" a value?

Eigenvectors represent a direction, not just a single point. When you have $x = -2y$, you have infinitely many solutions. We choose $y=1$ as a "free variable" to find the simplest vector on that line. You could pick $y=5$ and get $x=-10$; it would still be the same eigenvector direction!
