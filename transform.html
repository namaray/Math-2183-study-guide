Geometric Matrix Transformations

Detailed guide on how matrices map points in 2D space[cite: 666, 861].

1. Standard Transformation Matrices

Every linear transformation $T(x,y)$ can be represented by a standard matrix $A$ such that $T(x) = Ax$[cite: 674, 862].

Transformation Standard Matrix ($2 \times 2$) Effect on $(x, y)$
Reflection (x-axis) $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$ $(x, -y)$ [cite: 698, 722, 874]
Reflection (y-axis) $\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$ $(-x, y)$ [cite: 687, 720, 876]
Reflection ($y=x$) $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $(y, x)$ [cite: 708, 879]
Reflection ($y=-x$) $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $(-y, -x)$ [cite: 714, 881]
Rotation (90° CW) $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $(y, -x)$ [cite: 762, 800]
Rotation (90° ACW) $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $(-y, x)$ [cite: 748, 903]

2. Worked Example: Point Reflection (Prob 21)

Find the image of $A(2,3)$ and $B(-1,4)$ when reflected along the y-axis [cite: 113-115, 715-717].

Point A(2, 3):
$\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (-1)(2) + (0)(3) \\ (0)(2) + (1)(3) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$
Image: A'(-2, 3) [cite: 727, 736]
Point B(-1, 4):
$\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (-1)(-1) + (0)(4) \\ (0)(-1) + (1)(4) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$
Image: B'(1, 4) [cite: 728, 737]

3. Worked Example: Triangle Enlargement (Prob 23)

Triangle with vertices $A(-2,3), B(3,3), C(3,5)$ mapped by scale factor $k = -2$ [cite: 125, 813, 817-822].

Rule: Multiply both coordinates by the scale factor $k$.
Matrix Form: $\begin{bmatrix} -2 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}$[cite: 828, 830].
Vertex A(-2, 3): $(-2 \cdot -2, -2 \cdot 3) = (4, -6)$ [cite: 857]
Vertex B(3, 3): $(-2 \cdot 3, -2 \cdot 3) = (-6, -6)$ [cite: 858]
Vertex C(3, 5): $(-2 \cdot 3, -2 \cdot 5) = (-6, -10)$ [cite: 859]
